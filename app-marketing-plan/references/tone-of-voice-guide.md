## 1. Voice vs. Tone

**Voice** is your brand's consistent personality. It does not change across channels, seasons, or campaigns. Voice is who you are -- the character traits that show up every time you write, whether it is a push notification or a billboard. Think of voice as a person's fundamental personality: someone who is dry and witty at a dinner party is still dry and witty at a job interview, even if they dial it back.

**Tone** is the emotional inflection you apply to your voice depending on context. A support message after a failed payment needs empathy. A celebration screen after a user hits a streak needs energy. Both should still sound like *you*, but the emotional register shifts.

### How This Works in Practice

**Slack's voice** is helpful, human, and slightly playful. That voice stays constant. But watch the tone shift:

- **Onboarding**: "You're in! Here's where the magic happens. Channels keep conversations organized -- think of them as rooms for every topic." (Warm, encouraging, guiding)
- **Error state**: "Something went wrong on our end. We're looking into it -- try again in a few minutes." (Calm, accountable, reassuring)
- **Release notes**: "Huddles just got an upgrade. Screen sharing is now sharper, faster, and works on mobile. Go ahead, show your work." (Confident, casual, product-proud)

**Headspace's voice** is calm, warm, and gently encouraging. The tone shifts:

- **Morning notification**: "Good morning. A few minutes of stillness can shape your whole day." (Soft, inviting)
- **Missed streak**: "No worries. There's no such thing as falling behind. Today is a fresh start." (Compassionate, no guilt)
- **Subscription prompt**: "Your free trial gave you a taste. A membership opens the full library -- hundreds of meditations, sleep sounds, and focus exercises." (Clear, generous, low-pressure)

**Duolingo's voice** is playful, bold, and a little cheeky. The tone shifts:

- **Lesson complete**: "Nailed it! You just learned 5 new words. Your future self thanks you." (Celebratory, fun)
- **Streak reminder**: "Your streak is about to break. Duo is watching." (Humorous urgency)
- **Error in exercise**: "Not quite! The correct answer is below. You'll get it next time." (Supportive, light)

The key insight: you should be able to read any piece of copy from your app and immediately recognize the brand behind it, even without seeing the logo.

## 2. Voice Attribute Framework

Define your voice with a "We are / We are not" table. This is the single most useful artifact in your voice toolkit. It draws boundaries. "Friendly" alone is meaningless -- every brand claims to be friendly. "Friendly but not sycophantic" tells your writers exactly where the line is.

### Real Brand Voice Attribute Tables

**Notion** -- The calm, capable tool:

| We are | We are not |
|--------|------------|
| Clear | Dumbed down |
| Capable | Intimidating |
| Calm | Flat or lifeless |
| Thoughtful | Slow or hesitant |
| Empowering | Preachy |

**Linear** -- The precision instrument:

| We are | We are not |
|--------|------------|
| Direct | Blunt or cold |
| Opinionated | Dismissive |
| Precise | Pedantic |
| Confident | Boastful |
| Fast-moving | Careless |

**Duolingo** -- The encouraging friend:

| We are | We are not |
|--------|------------|
| Playful | Silly or childish |
| Bold | Aggressive |
| Encouraging | Patronizing |
| Irreverent | Disrespectful |
| Inclusive | Generic |

**Bear (writing app)** -- The quiet craftsperson:

| We are | We are not |
|--------|------------|
| Minimal | Empty |
| Warm | Bubbly |
| Refined | Pretentious |
| Understated | Invisible |
| Thoughtful | Overthinking |

### Writing Operational Definitions

Each attribute needs an operational definition that tells writers *how* to embody it. Without this, attributes are just adjectives on a slide deck.

- **Clear** (Notion): We use short sentences and common words. We break complex ideas into steps. We never assume the reader already knows how something works. We explain, but we do not over-explain.
- **Direct** (Linear): We get to the point in the first sentence. We do not pad with pleasantries or hedge with qualifiers. We say what we mean. If something is broken, we say it is broken.
- **Playful** (Duolingo): We use humor when it reduces tension, not when it distracts. We write like a witty friend, not a comedian doing a set. Wordplay is welcome. Sarcasm is not.
- **Minimal** (Bear): We say what needs to be said and nothing more. Every word earns its place. We trust the reader to fill in the gaps. Silence is part of our voice.
- **Empowering** (Notion): We position the user as capable. We say "you can" instead of "we let you." The product is the tool; the user is the builder.
- **Confident** (Linear): We state our position without apology. We do not say "we think" or "we believe" -- we just say it. But we back opinions with reasoning.

## 3. Formality Spectrum

Position your brand on the spectrum below. Most mobile apps live between casual and professional. Pick one level as your default, then define when you shift up or down.

### Very Casual
Best for: lifestyle apps, social platforms, apps targeting Gen Z or millennial audiences.

> Your report's ready -- go check it out. We crunched the numbers so you don't have to. Spoiler: you crushed it this week. Three new personal bests and zero excuses needed. Share it with your friends or keep it as your little secret. Either way, we're proud of you.

Apps that use this level well: Duolingo, Cash App, Robinhood (early days).

### Casual
Best for: productivity tools aimed at individuals, creative apps, fitness apps.

> Your weekly report is ready! You had a strong week -- three personal bests and consistent daily activity. Take a look at the breakdown in your dashboard. If you want to share your progress, there's a share button at the top.

Apps that use this level well: Slack, Todoist, Strava.

### Conversational
Best for: SaaS tools, mid-market B2B, apps serving professionals who value clarity over personality.

> Your weekly report is ready. This week showed strong progress: three personal records and consistent daily engagement. View the full breakdown in your dashboard. You can also export or share the report from the top menu.

Apps that use this level well: Notion, Figma, Craft.

### Professional
Best for: finance, health, enterprise tools where trust and precision matter more than personality.

> Your weekly performance report has been generated. Key highlights include three personal records and consistent daily activity metrics. The full report is available in your dashboard, with options to export or share via the top navigation menu.

Apps that use this level well: Linear, Mercury, 1Password.

### Formal
Best for: legal, medical, government, or regulatory contexts. Rarely appropriate for an entire app, but sometimes necessary for specific screens like terms, privacy, or compliance.

> Your weekly performance report has been successfully generated and is now available for review. The report contains updated metrics reflecting three new personal records alongside daily activity data. Please access the full report through your dashboard. Export and sharing functionality is available via the primary navigation.

Apps that use this level: banking apps (compliance sections), health record apps, enterprise audit tools.

### Choosing Your Default

Your default formality level should match the most common interaction your user has with your app. Then define your range -- how far up or down you move for specific contexts. For example:

- **Default**: Casual
- **Shift up to Conversational**: Help docs, in-app education
- **Shift up to Professional**: Billing, account security, data privacy
- **Shift down to Very Casual**: Achievement celebrations, referral prompts, empty states

## 4. Tone Shifts by Context

Same voice, different tone. Each channel has a different job, and the tone should serve that job.

### App Store Listing
**Purpose**: Attract and convert. **Tone**: Confident, benefit-focused, slightly aspirational.

Lead with the outcome, not the feature. The App Store is a storefront. Write like a great product label, not a spec sheet.

- Strong: "The to-do list that actually gets things done. Todoist helps 30 million people organize work and life."
- Strong: "Your journal, locked tight. Day One keeps your private thoughts private with end-to-end encryption."
- Weak: "A powerful task management application with cross-platform syncing, natural language processing, and 80+ integrations."

### In-App Paywall
**Purpose**: Upgrade conversion. **Tone**: Helpful, value-oriented, zero pressure.

Show what they gain. Make saying "no" easy and guilt-free. Never create artificial urgency.

- Strong: "You've used 3 of your 5 free projects. Upgrade to Pro for unlimited projects, priority support, and advanced exports. Or keep your free plan -- it's not going anywhere."
- Strong: "Go Pro and unlock everything. Unlimited meditations. Personalized plans. Offline access. Try it free for 7 days -- cancel anytime."
- Weak: "UPGRADE NOW to unlock premium features! Don't miss out on this limited-time offer!!!"

### Social Media
**Purpose**: Engage and grow audience. **Tone**: Authentic, personality-forward, educational.

Share insights, not pitches. Be the account people follow for the content, not despite the promotions.

- Strong: "Unpopular opinion: your to-do list doesn't need more features. It needs fewer items."
- Strong: "We asked 1,000 remote workers what kills their focus. #1 answer: not meetings. Context switching."
- Weak: "Excited to announce our new feature! Check it out now at [link]!"

### Email Newsletter
**Purpose**: Nurture relationship. **Tone**: Personal, generous, story-driven.

Write like you are emailing one person. Open with something real, not a marketing hook.

- Strong: "Last Tuesday I spent 45 minutes looking for a file I'd saved three days earlier. That's when I realized our search needed a complete rethink. Here's what we changed."
- Weak: "We're thrilled to announce exciting new updates to our platform! Read on to discover what's new."

### Support / Error Messages
**Purpose**: Help and reassure. **Tone**: Empathetic, clear, solution-oriented.

Acknowledge the frustration first, then provide the fix. Never blame the user.

- Strong: "That didn't work. The file might be too large -- try one under 25MB, or compress it first."
- Strong: "We can't connect to your bank right now. This usually fixes itself within an hour. We'll notify you when it's back."
- Weak: "Error 403: Access Denied. The requested resource could not be retrieved."

### Landing Page
**Purpose**: Convert visitors. **Tone**: Clear, specific, proof-heavy.

Every sentence should move the reader toward the call to action. Cut anything that does not serve conversion.

- Strong: "4,200 design teams use Figma to ship faster. Not because it has more features -- because it gets out of the way."
- Weak: "Figma is an innovative, best-in-class design platform that empowers teams to collaborate seamlessly."

## 5. App-Specific Voice Contexts

Mobile apps have unique touchpoints that websites and print materials do not. These micro-moments are where voice either builds trust and delight or creates friction.

### Onboarding Flows
The first interaction sets expectations for the entire relationship. Be clear about what the user needs to do and warm about why it matters.

- **Headspace**: "Let's find your starting point. Are you here for stress, sleep, or focus? (You can change this later.)"
- **Duolingo**: "What language do you want to learn? Pick one to start -- you can add more anytime."
- **Notion**: "What will you use Notion for? Pick what feels right. We'll set up your workspace to match."

Principles: minimize choices, reduce anxiety about commitment ("you can change this later"), show immediate value.

### Push Notifications
The most intimate and invasive touchpoint. Every notification is an interruption. Earn it.

- **Effective**: "Your flight to London is delayed 40 minutes. New departure: 3:15 PM." (Useful, timely, specific)
- **Effective**: "Sarah commented on your design: 'Love the header layout.'" (Social, actionable)
- **Effective**: "You're 2 minutes from completing today's lesson." (Encouraging, low-commitment)
- **Ineffective**: "Hey! We miss you. Come back and check out what's new!" (Guilt-trip, vague)
- **Ineffective**: "Don't forget to open the app today!" (No value, no reason)

Rule of thumb: if the notification does not contain information the user actually needs or wants right now, do not send it.

### Empty States
An empty screen is a missed opportunity. Use it to guide, motivate, or delight.

- **Notion** (empty page): "Press Enter to continue with an empty page, or pick a template."
- **Bear** (no notes): "Your notes will appear here. Tap the + button to start writing."
- **Linear** (no issues): "No issues yet. Create your first issue to start tracking work."
- **Duolingo** (no friends): "Add friends to see how you stack up. Learning is better with a little competition."

Principles: tell the user what to do next, keep the tone aligned with your voice, never leave a screen feeling broken or abandoned.

### Error Messages
Errors are stressful. Your voice should de-escalate, not add confusion.

Structure: (1) What happened, (2) Why it happened (if you know), (3) What to do next.

- "We couldn't save your changes. Check your internet connection and try again."
- "Something went wrong loading your feed. Pull down to refresh, or we'll try again in a moment."
- "That password doesn't match our records. Try again, or reset it -- we'll send a link to your email."

Never say: "An unexpected error occurred" (all errors are unexpected to the user), "Invalid input" (clinical and unhelpful), "Please try again later" (without explaining why or when).

### Achievement Celebrations
These moments build habit loops. Match the energy of the accomplishment without being over the top.

- **Small win**: "Nice! Lesson complete." (Brief, warm)
- **Medium milestone**: "10 days in a row. You're building a real habit." (Acknowledges effort)
- **Major achievement**: "100-day streak. That's not luck -- that's dedication. You've completed more lessons than 97% of learners." (Specific, earned, celebratory)

Avoid: excessive exclamation marks, generic praise ("Amazing!!!"), confetti on trivial actions.

## 6. Vocabulary Guide

### Preferred Words
Words that reinforce your brand personality. Build a list of at least 20 preferred words and their alternatives.

| Instead of | Use | Why |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| utilize | use | Natural, not corporate |
| streamline | simplify | Concrete, not jargon |
| users | people | Human, not clinical |
| leverage | use, build on | Overused buzzword |
| empower | help, let, enable | Vague corporate-speak |
| facilitate | help, make easier | Bureaucratic |
| robust | strong, reliable | Tech buzzword |
| seamless | smooth, easy | Overused, meaningless |
| innovative | new, different, better | Every company says this |
| disruptive | -- (describe what changed) | Meaningless without specifics |
| best-in-class | -- (show proof instead) | Unverifiable claim |
| cutting-edge | modern, latest | Cliche |
| solution | tool, app, product | Vague |
| stakeholders | team, customers, people | Corporate jargon |
| synergy | -- (describe the actual benefit) | The canonical buzzword |
| ecosystem | tools, platform, integrations | Overused in tech |
| paradigm | approach, model | Academic jargon |
| actionable | useful, practical | Buzzy |
| bandwidth | time, capacity | Metaphor creep |
| circle back | follow up, revisit | Corporate slang |

### Banned Words and Phrases
Words that contradict most app brand voices:

**Buzzwords**: synergy, leverage, disrupt, revolutionary, game-changer, next-generation, bleeding-edge, world-class, turn-key, end-to-end, holistic, scalable (unless discussing infrastructure)

**Hedging**: arguably, relatively, somewhat, perhaps, it could be said that, in some ways

**Superlatives without proof**: best, fastest, most powerful, ultimate, number one, leading, premier

**False urgency**: act now, limited time, don't miss out, hurry, before it's too late (unless the deadline is real and specific)

**Filler phrases**: in order to (use "to"), at this point in time (use "now"), in the event that (use "if"), due to the fact that (use "because"), it should be noted that (delete entirely)

### Jargon Policy

- **App Store copy and marketing pages**: Zero jargon. Write for someone who has never heard of your product category.
- **Onboarding**: Minimal jargon. Explain any technical term on first use.
- **In-app for regular users**: Some jargon is fine if your audience uses it daily. A design app can say "kerning." A finance app can say "P/E ratio."
- **Power-user features and docs**: Jargon expected. Using simple terms here would feel condescending.

The test: read your copy aloud to someone outside your industry. If they look confused, simplify.

## 7. Example Rewrite Framework

Take generic sentences and rewrite them in different brand voices. This exercise calibrates your team on what your voice actually sounds like in practice.

### Rewrite Set 1: Feature Announcement

**Generic**: "Our application utilizes advanced algorithms to optimize your workflow efficiency."

| Brand Voice | Rewrite |
|-------------|---------|
| Slack (helpful, human) | "We built some smart shortcuts into your workflow. Less busywork, more actual work." |
| Headspace (calm, warm) | "Your app now learns your rhythms and gently removes what's getting in the way." |
| Duolingo (playful, bold) | "Our robots learned your routine and took out the boring parts. You're welcome." |
| Notion (clear, empowering) | "New: intelligent workflows. The app adapts to how you work and automates repetitive steps." |
| Linear (direct, precise) | "Automated workflow optimization. The system detects repeated patterns and eliminates manual steps." |
| Bear (minimal, warm) | "Your workflow, simplified. The app handles the repetitive parts quietly." |

### Rewrite Set 2: Upgrade Prompt

**Generic**: "Upgrade to our premium plan to access advanced features and enhanced functionality."

| Brand Voice | Rewrite |
|-------------|---------|
| Slack (helpful, human) | "Ready for more? Pro gives you unlimited message history, group calls, and custom integrations. Try it free for 30 days." |
| Headspace (calm, warm) | "Your free sessions are just the beginning. A membership opens the full library -- hundreds of meditations, sleep stories, and guided exercises." |
| Duolingo (playful, bold) | "Free is great. Super is better. No ads, unlimited hearts, and access to every course. Your future polyglot self will thank you." |
| Notion (clear, empowering) | "Upgrade to Team. Unlimited blocks, advanced permissions, and admin tools for growing workspaces." |
| Linear (direct, precise) | "Pro plan: unlimited issues, priority support, advanced analytics. $8/user/month." |
| Bear (minimal, warm) | "Bear Pro: all themes, all export options, and sync across every device. A small upgrade for a better writing experience." |

### Rewrite Set 3: Error Message

**Generic**: "An error has occurred. Please try again later."

| Brand Voice | Rewrite |
|-------------|---------|
| Slack | "Something went wrong on our end. We're looking into it. Try again in a few minutes." |
| Headspace | "That didn't go as planned. Take a breath -- we're working on it. Try again shortly." |
| Duolingo | "Oops. Something broke. (It wasn't your Spanish.) We're fixing it now." |
| Notion | "We couldn't complete that action. Try refreshing the page. If it persists, check our status page." |
| Linear | "Request failed. Retrying automatically. Check status.linear.app if this continues." |
| Bear | "Something went wrong. Try again in a moment." |

## 8. Voice-to-Visual Design

Voice and visual design are two expressions of the same brand personality. When they contradict each other, users feel the disconnect even if they cannot articulate it.

### How Voice Attributes Map to Design

| Voice Attribute | Visual Expression |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Playful | Rounded corners, bright colors, illustrations, bouncy animations |
| Minimal | Generous whitespace, limited color palette, no decorative elements |
| Professional | Clean grids, neutral palette, sharp corners, subtle transitions |
| Warm | Soft colors, organic shapes, handwritten elements, photography over illustrations |
| Bold | High contrast, large type, strong color blocking, confident motion |
| Calm | Muted palette, slow transitions, ample breathing room, serif or thin sans-serif type |

### Typography and Voice

- **Very casual voice** pairs with rounded sans-serifs, variable weights, and generous tracking. Think Inter, Nunito, Quicksand.
- **Conversational voice** pairs with clean geometric sans-serifs. Think SF Pro, Helvetica Neue, Instrument Sans.
- **Professional voice** pairs with precise, neutral typefaces with strong readability. Think Inter, Suisse, Untitled Sans.
- **Formal voice** pairs with serifs or conservative sans-serifs. Think Georgia, Tiempos, Times New Roman.

### Motion and Voice

- A playful voice justifies bouncy, spring-based animations and confetti effects.
- A calm voice calls for slow fades, gentle easing, and minimal motion.
- A direct voice means instant transitions -- no unnecessary animation between states.
- A warm voice favors organic motion curves and subtle parallax effects.

If your copy says "Simple. Fast. Done." but your page loads with a 3-second cinematic animation, the voice and visuals are fighting each other.

## 9. Voice Audit and Consistency

### How to Audit Existing Copy

Run this audit quarterly or after any major rebrand or voice update.

**Step 1: Collect samples.** Pull 15-20 pieces of live copy from across your touchpoints: App Store description, onboarding screens, push notifications, error messages, paywall, marketing emails, social posts, help docs.

**Step 2: Blind read.** Remove logos and branding. Read each piece aloud. Ask: does this sound like the same person wrote it? Could you tell it's your brand without seeing the name?

**Step 3: Score against attributes.** For each piece, rate (1-5) how well it reflects each of your voice attributes. A piece that scores 5/5 on "clear" but 1/5 on "warm" means your clarity is consistent but your warmth is not.

**Step 4: Find the outliers.** Which pieces feel off-brand? Common offenders: legal copy (too formal), old help articles (pre-rebrand voice), error messages (too technical), push notifications (too aggressive).

**Step 5: Rewrite the worst offenders.** Pick the 5 pieces that scored lowest and rewrite them. These become before/after examples for your team.

**Step 6: Update the guide.** If you found new patterns (good or bad) during the audit, add them to your voice guide as examples.

### Consistency Checklist

After writing any deliverable, verify:

- [ ] The same personality comes through in every document
- [ ] Formality level is consistent -- no sudden shifts from casual to corporate
- [ ] Vocabulary choices match the preferred/banned word lists
- [ ] Tone shifts are intentional and context-appropriate, not accidental
- [ ] Brand personality is evident in the writing, not just stated as a claim
- [ ] Sentence structure matches your voice (short and punchy vs. flowing and descriptive)
- [ ] CTAs sound like you, not like generic SaaS copy
- [ ] Error messages and edge cases got the same voice attention as hero copy
- [ ] Empty states, loading states, and confirmation dialogs are on-brand
- [ ] Push notification copy would pass the "would I be annoyed receiving this?" test

## 10. Common Voice Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Voice Without Boundaries
Saying "we're friendly and approachable" without defining what that *excludes*. Every brand claims to be friendly. The "We are not" column is where the real differentiation happens.

**Fix**: Always define your voice in pairs. "Friendly but not sycophantic." "Confident but not arrogant." "Minimal but not cold."

### Mistake 2: Hero Copy Gets the Love, Everything Else Gets Neglected
Teams spend hours on the App Store headline and zero minutes on error messages, empty states, and confirmation dialogs. Users spend far more time with those micro-moments than with your hero banner.

**Fix**: Apply your voice guide to every touchpoint, especially the overlooked ones. Write your error messages with the same care as your headlines.

### Mistake 3: Copying Another Brand's Voice
Admiring Duolingo's personality and importing it into a finance app. Voice must match your product category, your audience's expectations, and your actual company personality.

**Fix**: Use other brands as inspiration for *how thoroughly* they apply their voice, not *what* their voice sounds like. Study the structure, not the style.

### Mistake 4: Confusing Informal with Unprofessional
Casual voice does not mean sloppy, grammatically incorrect, or vague. Slack is casual and precise. Duolingo is playful and clear.

**Fix**: Casual is a formality choice. Clarity is non-negotiable at every formality level.

### Mistake 5: Using Exclamation Points as a Substitute for Energy
"Your report is ready!" "You did it!" "Welcome!" "Great choice!" Exclamation points everywhere signal forced enthusiasm, not genuine energy.

**Fix**: Write sentences that are energetic by their content and word choice, not by punctuation. "You just nailed a 10-day streak" has more energy than "Congratulations!!!"

### Mistake 6: Writing for Your Team, Not Your User
Using internal jargon ("onboarding flow," "premium tier," "engagement metric") in user-facing copy. Your user does not know or care about your internal terminology.

**Fix**: Read every piece of user-facing copy from the perspective of someone who downloaded your app five minutes ago.

### Mistake 7: Tone-Deaf Urgency
"Your streak is about to die!" or "Don't lose your progress!" in a meditation app. Urgency works for Duolingo because their voice supports it. It backfires when it contradicts your brand's personality.

**Fix**: Match urgency tactics to your voice attributes. A calm brand uses gentle reminders. A bold brand uses direct urgency. Neither uses guilt.

### Mistake 8: Ignoring Cultural Context
Humor, idioms, and references that work in American English may confuse or offend users in other markets. "Knock it out of the park" means nothing in countries without baseball.

**Fix**: If your app serves international users, avoid culturally specific idioms. Test copy with native speakers in your key markets.

### Mistake 9: Letting AI-Generated Copy Ship Unedited
AI can generate competent first drafts but tends toward generic, hedge-heavy, buzzword-laden copy. "Elevate your productivity with our innovative solution" is what AI produces when given no voice constraints.

**Fix**: Use AI for drafts, but always edit through your voice guide. Score AI output against your "We are / We are not" table before publishing.

### Mistake 10: No Single Source of Truth
Voice guidelines live in a PDF no one reads, a Notion page no one bookmarks, and a Slack message from 2023 no one can find.

**Fix**: Keep your voice guide in one canonical location. Link to it from your content briefs. Review it in every copy kickoff meeting. Update it when you learn something new.
