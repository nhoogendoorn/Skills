## 1. Pre-Writing Context Framework

Every piece of marketing copy exists in a context. Writing without understanding that context is like prescribing medicine without a diagnosis -- you might get lucky, but you will probably do harm. Gather the following information before writing a single word.

### Page Purpose

- **What type of page or surface is this?** A homepage serves a fundamentally different audience than a paid landing page. An app store listing operates under strict character limits and a scan-heavy reading pattern. A paywall screen has roughly three seconds to justify a purchase. Each surface has its own constraints, and your copy must be shaped by them.
- **What is the ONE primary action visitors should take?** Every page should have a single primary goal. If you try to get someone to sign up, download, share, and read your blog all at once, they will do none of those things. Decide the one thing that matters most, and make everything else subordinate.
- **What is the secondary action?** For visitors who are not ready for the primary action, what is the softer alternative? On a landing page this might be "Watch a demo" instead of "Start free trial." On a paywall this might be "Continue with limited features."

### Audience

- **Who is the ideal customer?** Be as specific as possible. "Busy professionals" is too broad. "Marketing managers at SaaS companies with 20-100 employees who manage 3+ social media channels" gives you something to write to.
- **What problem are they trying to solve?** Not the abstract, CEO-pitch version. The daily, tangible pain. Not "they need better analytics" but "they spend every Monday morning manually pulling numbers from four dashboards into a spreadsheet their boss never reads."
- **What have they already tried?** This tells you what to position against. If they have tried spreadsheets, your copy should address why your solution is not "just another spreadsheet." If they have tried a competitor, you need to address switching costs.
- **What objections or hesitations do they have?** Common ones: "This seems expensive," "I do not have time to learn a new tool," "I have been burned before," "This probably will not work for my specific situation." Your copy needs to neutralize these.
- **What language do they use?** Pull exact phrases from app store reviews, support tickets, Reddit threads, and customer interviews. When someone reads their own words in your copy, trust goes up immediately.

### Product/Offer

- **What are you selling or offering?** Be precise about what the customer gets. Not "a productivity app" but "a daily planner that syncs with your calendar and sends smart reminders based on your habits."
- **What makes it different from alternatives?** If you cannot answer this in one sentence, your positioning needs work before your copy does.
- **What is the key transformation or outcome?** People do not buy apps. They buy the version of themselves that uses the app. What does that version look like?
- **Proof points?** Numbers, testimonials, case studies, awards, press mentions, app store ratings. Proof is what separates claims from credibility.

### Traffic Context

- **Where is traffic coming from?** Someone clicking an Instagram ad has a very different mindset than someone who Googled "best habit tracking app 2026." The ad clicker is interruption-based (you need to earn attention fast). The searcher is intent-based (they are already looking for what you offer).
- **What do visitors already know?** If they just watched a 60-second video ad, do not repeat everything from the ad. Build on it. If they are coming from organic search, assume they know nothing.
- **What messaging did they see before this page?** Message match matters enormously. If your ad says "Track habits in 30 seconds a day" and your landing page headline says "The All-In-One Productivity Platform," you have just broken the visitor's trust.

## 2. Core Copywriting Principles

### Clarity Over Cleverness

This is the most important principle in this entire guide. Clever copy wins awards. Clear copy wins customers.

**Why this matters:** Your reader is scanning, distracted, and skeptical. They will not pause to decode a pun. They will not re-read a sentence to figure out what you meant. If they do not understand your message in the first pass, they leave.

**Before (clever but unclear):**
> Unlock the symphony of your daily rhythm.

**After (clear and compelling):**
> Plan your day in 2 minutes. Stay focused for 8 hours.

**Before:**
> Where innovation meets your morning routine.

**After:**
> The morning planner that actually sticks. Used by 50,000 people who never stuck with a planner before.

**App store example -- before:**
> Revolutionizing the paradigm of personal finance management through intelligent automation.

**App store example -- after:**
> See where your money goes. Set budgets that work. Save without thinking about it.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using metaphors that require industry knowledge ("We are the Salesforce of meal planning")
- Writing headlines that could apply to any product in any category
- Prioritizing rhythm or rhyme over meaning
- Using internal product names or jargon that customers do not recognize

**The test:** Read your copy to someone unfamiliar with your product. If they cannot tell you what you are offering and why they should care, rewrite it.

### Benefits Over Features

Features describe what your product does. Benefits describe what that means for the customer. Outcomes describe the transformation in their life. You need all three, but lead with benefits and outcomes.

**Why this matters:** People do not buy features. They buy solutions to their problems and improvements to their lives. A feature is a fact about your product. A benefit is a fact about what your product does for the customer. The gap between these two is where most app copy fails.

**The Feature-Benefit-Outcome chain:**

| Feature | Benefit | Outcome |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Push notifications | Never forget a task | Feel in control of your day |
| End-to-end encryption | Your data stays private | Peace of mind about sensitive info |
| Offline mode | Works without internet | Use it on the subway, on planes, anywhere |
| AI-powered suggestions | Less time deciding, more time doing | Finish your to-do list before lunch |

**Before (feature-focused app store description):**
> Features: Cloud sync, dark mode, widgets, Siri shortcuts, Apple Watch app, Calendar integration, 50+ templates.

**After (benefit-focused app store description):**
> Your plan follows you everywhere. Sync across every device, check tasks from your wrist, and add to-dos with your voice. Start in seconds with templates built for how you actually work.

**In-app example -- before:**
> This feature uses machine learning to analyze your patterns.

**In-app example -- after:**
> We learn when you are most productive and schedule your hardest tasks for those hours.

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing features in bullet points without connecting them to real-world use
- Using the word "powerful" as a substitute for explaining why something matters
- Describing technical implementation instead of user impact ("Built with SwiftUI" means nothing to most users)

### Specificity Over Vagueness

Vague copy feels like marketing. Specific copy feels like truth. The more specific you are, the more believable and persuasive your copy becomes.

**Why this matters:** Specificity activates the reader's imagination. "Save time" is abstract. "Get your mornings back" is slightly better. "Finish your morning routine in 12 minutes instead of 45" is vivid -- the reader can see it.

**Before/after examples:**

| Vague | Specific |
|-------|----------|
| Save time on your workflow | Cut your weekly reporting from 4 hours to 15 minutes |
| Trusted by thousands | Used by 12,847 teams including Shopify, Notion, and Linear |
| Fast performance | App loads in 0.3 seconds. No spinners. No waiting. |
| Affordable pricing | Free for personal use. $4/month for everything else. |
| Easy to use | Set up in 2 minutes. No account required. |
| Improve your health | Users walk an average of 2,300 more steps per day |

**App store example -- before:**
> The best meditation app for stress relief and mindfulness.

**App store example -- after:**
> 10-minute guided sessions designed for people who have never meditated. 73% of users report lower stress after one week.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using "up to" claims ("Save up to 10 hours a week") that readers mentally discount to zero
- Rounding numbers unnecessarily (say "12,847 users" not "thousands of users")
- Making big claims without evidence ("The #1 app for..." without a source)

### Customer Language Over Company Language

Your customers describe their problems differently than you describe your solution. Their language is more emotional, more concrete, and more persuasive than anything you can invent.

**Why this matters:** When someone reads their own words in your copy, the internal response is "This person understands me." That response is the foundation of trust, and trust is the foundation of conversion.

**Where to find customer language:**
- App store reviews (yours and competitors')
- Support tickets and help desk conversations
- Reddit threads, Twitter/X posts, forum discussions
- Customer interview transcripts
- Sales call recordings
- Cancellation survey responses

**Before (company language):**
> Our intelligent task prioritization engine leverages contextual signals to surface high-impact items.

**After (customer language):**
> It tells you what to work on next. No more staring at a list of 47 tasks wondering where to start.

**Before (company language):**
> Seamlessly integrate your productivity ecosystem for enhanced cross-platform collaboration.

**After (customer language):**
> Your notes, tasks, and calendar in one place. Stop switching between six apps to plan your day.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using internal feature names customers have never heard of
- Describing the technology instead of the experience
- Writing for your CEO or investors instead of your users
- Assuming customers care about how it works (they care about what it does for them)

### One Idea Per Section

Each section of your page, each screen in your onboarding, each paragraph in your app store listing should advance exactly one argument or idea.

**Why this matters:** Attention is sequential. A reader can hold one new idea at a time. When you combine "we are fast AND secure AND affordable AND used by big companies" into a single section, none of those claims gets the weight it deserves.

**Before (overloaded section):**
> FocusApp helps you build better habits, track your time, manage projects, collaborate with your team, and integrate with your favorite tools -- all with enterprise-grade security and a beautiful interface.

**After (one idea per section):**

Section 1 -- Hero: "Build one habit at a time. FocusApp tracks your streaks and keeps you honest."

Section 2 -- Social proof: "Joined by 50,000 people building better routines."

Section 3 -- How it works: Three steps.

Section 4 -- Deeper benefit: "Not just tracking -- understanding. See which habits stick and why."

**Common mistakes:**
- Hero sections that try to mention every feature
- App store descriptions that read like a feature changelog
- Paywall screens listing 12 benefits (pick the top 3)

## 3. Writing Style Rules

These rules apply to all marketing copy -- landing pages, app store descriptions, in-app messaging, paywall screens, push notifications, and email.

### 1. Simple Over Complex

Use everyday words. Your goal is communication, not sophistication.

**Why this matters:** Reading complex language requires cognitive effort. Cognitive effort creates friction. Friction kills conversion. Research consistently shows that simpler language is perceived as more trustworthy and more credible.

| Complex | Simple |
|---------|--------|
| Utilize | Use |
| Facilitate | Help |
| Leverage | Use |
| Implement | Set up |
| Commence | Start |
| Terminate | End |
| Endeavor | Try |
| Subsequent | Next |
| Paramount | Important |
| Functionality | Feature |

**Before:**
> Commence your wellness endeavor by utilizing our comprehensive meditation functionality.

**After:**
> Start meditating today. Pick a session and press play.

**App store before:**
> Facilitate seamless collaboration and communication amongst your team members with our enterprise-grade productivity solution.

**App store after:**
> Work together without the chaos. One app for tasks, chat, and files.

### 2. Specific Over Vague

Avoid words that sound impressive but communicate nothing. These words are the empty calories of copywriting.

**Banned word list with alternatives:**

| Vague Word | What to Write Instead |
|------------|----------------------|
| Streamline | Describe the specific improvement: "Cut onboarding from 3 days to 3 hours" |
| Optimize | Name what gets better: "Reduce your ad spend by 23%" |
| Innovative | Describe what is actually new: "The first app that scans receipts with your camera" |
| Cutting-edge | Explain the technology in plain terms: "Uses on-device AI so your data never leaves your phone" |
| Robust | Describe the capability: "Handles teams of 2 to 2,000 without slowing down" |
| Seamless | Describe the experience: "Import your data in one click. No CSV files. No manual entry." |
| Next-generation | Explain what changed: "Rebuilt from scratch with 4x faster sync" |
| World-class | Provide proof: "Rated 4.8 stars by 23,000 users" |
| Powerful | Show the power: "Run 50 automations simultaneously" |
| Best-in-class | Prove it: "Fastest load time of any app in the category (benchmarked by TechCrunch)" |

### 3. Active Over Passive

Active voice is direct and energetic. Passive voice is indirect and lifeless.

**Why this matters:** Active voice makes it clear who is doing what. It is shorter, punchier, and easier to scan. Passive voice hides the actor, which in marketing copy either obscures your product's role or distances the reader from the action.

| Passive | Active |
|---------|--------|
| Reports are generated automatically | We generate your reports automatically |
| Your data is protected by encryption | We encrypt your data end-to-end |
| Tasks can be organized by priority | Organize your tasks by priority |
| Insights are delivered to your inbox | Get insights in your inbox every morning |
| Progress is tracked in real time | Track your progress in real time |

**Exception:** Passive voice is sometimes appropriate when the actor is irrelevant or when you want to emphasize the object. "Your account was created successfully" is fine because the focus is on the account, not who created it.

### 4. Confident Over Qualified

Hedging words weaken every sentence they touch. Remove them unless you have a legal reason to keep them.

**Why this matters:** Hedging signals uncertainty. If you are not confident in your own product, why should the reader be?

**Words to cut or replace:**

| Hedging | Confident |
|---------|-----------|
| Our product can help you save time | Save time with [Product] |
| You might find this useful for... | Use this to... |
| We believe our approach is effective | Our approach works. Here is proof. |
| This could potentially reduce... | This reduces... |
| It is basically a... | It is a... |
| Almost all users report... | 94% of users report... |
| Very fast performance | Loads in 0.3 seconds |
| Really easy to set up | Set up in 2 minutes |

**Exception:** Be careful with absolute claims you cannot prove. "Our app will make you productive" is a promise you cannot guarantee. "Our app helped 73% of users complete more tasks" is specific and defensible.

### 5. Show Over Tell

Do not tell readers your product is "fast" or "easy." Show them what fast and easy look like in practice.

**Why this matters:** Every app claims to be fast, easy, and powerful. These words have been emptied of meaning by overuse. When you show instead of tell, you bypass the reader's skepticism filter.

| Telling | Showing |
|---------|---------|
| Lightning fast | Opens in 0.3 seconds. Try it. |
| Super easy to use | Tap, type, done. Three steps to your first task. |
| Incredibly powerful | Run 200 automations. Track 50 goals. Zero lag. |
| Beautiful design | [Show a screenshot -- the design speaks for itself] |
| Loved by users | "I deleted 4 apps after finding this one." -- Sarah K. |

**Before (telling):**
> Our amazingly intuitive interface makes it incredibly easy to instantly create beautiful presentations.

**After (showing):**
> Pick a template. Drop in your content. Present in 10 minutes. Your audience will think you spent hours.

### 6. Honest Over Sensational

Never fabricate statistics, claims, or testimonials. Never manufacture urgency. Never misrepresent your product's capabilities.

**Why this matters beyond ethics:** Dishonest copy erodes trust the moment someone discovers the deception. In the app world, this shows up as negative reviews, refund requests, and churn. One honest claim outperforms ten exaggerated ones because it is believable.

**Red flags to avoid:**
- "The #1 app for..." without a verifiable source
- Fake countdown timers or scarcity indicators
- Screenshots that show features your app does not have
- Testimonials you wrote yourself
- "As seen on..." logos for publications that never covered you
- "Limited time offer" that runs indefinitely

**Instead:** Let real results speak. If your numbers are modest, own them. "Used by 847 people who love it" is more trustworthy than "Trusted by thousands worldwide."

### Quick Quality Check

Before finalizing any piece of copy, run through this scan:

- **Jargon check:** Would someone outside your industry understand every word?
- **Overloaded sentences:** Does any sentence have more than three conjunctions (and, or, but)? Split it.
- **Passive voice:** Search for "is," "are," "was," "were" + past participle. Rewrite in active voice.
- **Exclamation points:** Remove them. All of them. If your copy needs an exclamation point to convey excitement, the words are not doing their job.
- **Buzzword audit:** Highlight every instance of "innovative," "seamless," "cutting-edge," "robust," "powerful," "leverage." Replace each one with a specific claim.
- **"We" vs "You" ratio:** Count instances of "we/our" versus "you/your." If "we" dominates, rewrite to center the customer.
- **So-what test:** After each sentence, ask "So what? Why should the reader care?" If you cannot answer, cut or rewrite.

## 4. Headline Formulas

Headlines do most of the heavy lifting. On a landing page, 80% of visitors read the headline. Only 20% read beyond it. In the app store, your title and subtitle are often the only text someone reads before deciding to look further or scroll past. Get the headline right and the rest of the page has a chance. Get it wrong and nothing else matters.

### Formula 1: {Achieve desirable outcome} without {pain point}

This formula works because it pairs a positive result with the removal of a negative. The reader gets what they want and avoids what they fear.

**Examples:**
- "Understand how users experience your site without drowning in numbers"
- "Learn a language without memorizing vocabulary lists"
- "Get fit without spending hours in the gym"
- "Manage your finances without spreadsheets or stress"
- "Build a meditation habit without forcing yourself to sit still"

**App store example:**
- Title: "Calm Budget"
- Subtitle: "Track spending without the anxiety"

### Formula 2: The {opposite of usual process} way to {achieve desirable outcome}

This creates curiosity by implying a contrarian or easier approach.

**Examples:**
- "The easiest way to turn your passion into income"
- "The laziest way to keep your house clean"
- "The fastest way to learn guitar -- without practicing scales"
- "The quiet way to build a massive audience"

### Formula 3: Never {unpleasant event} again

Direct and emotional. Best for problems that are recurring and frustrating.

**Examples:**
- "Never miss a sales opportunity again"
- "Never forget a birthday again"
- "Never lose a receipt again"
- "Never show up to a meeting unprepared again"
- "Never wonder where your money went again"

### Formula 4: {Key feature/product type} for {target audience}

Simple positioning. Tells people exactly what this is and who it is for.

**Examples:**
- "Advanced analytics for Shopify e-commerce"
- "A journal for people who hate journaling"
- "Meal planning for busy parents"
- "Project management for freelancers and solo creators"
- "A habit tracker for people who have tried every habit tracker"

### Formula 5: {Feature} for {audience} to {what it is used for}

Adds the use case, which helps the reader self-identify.

**Examples:**
- "An online whiteboard for teams to ideate and brainstorm together"
- "A daily planner for students to stay on top of deadlines"
- "A recipe app for home cooks to discover meals with what is already in the fridge"

### Formula 6: You don't have to {skills or resources} to {achieve desirable outcome}

Lowers the perceived barrier. Especially effective when your audience feels intimidated.

**Examples:**
- "You do not have to be an SEO pro to rank higher and get more traffic"
- "You do not have to be a designer to create stunning presentations"
- "You do not have to wake up at 5am to be productive"
- "You do not need a personal trainer to work out effectively"

### Formula 7: {Achieve desirable outcome} by {how product makes it possible}

Combines the what with the how. Gives the reader a reason to believe.

**Examples:**
- "Generate more leads by seeing which companies visit your site"
- "Sleep better by tracking the habits that actually affect your rest"
- "Save money by seeing every subscription you forgot about"

### Formula 8: {Key benefit of your product} -- short and direct

Sometimes the simplest approach wins. State the benefit plainly.

**Examples:**
- "Sound clear in online meetings"
- "Remember everything"
- "Know your numbers"
- "Ship faster"
- "Sleep better tonight"

### Formula 9: {Question highlighting the main pain point}

Questions engage readers because the brain cannot help but answer them internally.

**Examples:**
- "Hate returning stuff to Amazon?"
- "Still using spreadsheets to track your budget?"
- "Tired of apps that promise simplicity but deliver complexity?"
- "When was the last time you finished everything on your to-do list?"

### Formula 10: Turn {input} into {outcome}

Transformation framing. Shows the before and after.

**Examples:**
- "Turn your hard-earned sales into repeat customers"
- "Turn voice memos into polished blog posts"
- "Turn daily walks into a fitness habit"
- "Turn receipts into tax deductions"

### Additional Formulas

- **"[Achieve outcome] in [timeframe]"** -- "Build your first app in a weekend"
- **"The [category] that [key differentiator]"** -- "The to-do app that does not nag you"
- **"Stop [pain]. Start [pleasure]."** -- "Stop guessing. Start knowing."
- **"[Number] [people] use [product] to [outcome]"** -- "12,000 freelancers use Harvest to get paid faster"
- **"What if [desirable but unlikely scenario]?"** -- "What if your budgeting app actually made you excited to check your balance?"
- **"[Product]: [One-line value prop]"** -- "Notion: One workspace. Every team."

### Headline Writing Tips

- **Write 20 before picking one.** Your first headline is almost never your best. Quantity leads to quality.
- **Test the "bar test."** If you said your headline out loud to a friend at a bar, would they understand it and find it interesting?
- **Front-load the value.** Put the most important words at the beginning. Readers scan left to right and often do not finish.
- **Keep it under 12 words** for landing pages. Under 8 words for app store subtitles.
- **Avoid starting with your company name.** Lead with the value, not the brand.

## 5. CTA Guidelines

A CTA (call to action) is where strategy becomes action. Everything else on the page builds toward this moment. A weak CTA wastes all the persuasion work that came before it.

### Why Most CTAs Fail

Generic CTAs like "Submit," "Sign Up," and "Learn More" fail because they describe the action from the company's perspective, not the customer's. "Submit" tells the user what they do for you. "Get Your Free Plan" tells them what they get for themselves.

### Weak CTAs and Why They Are Weak

| Weak CTA | Why It Fails |
|----------|-------------|
| Submit | Sounds like homework. Tells the user nothing about what happens next. |
| Sign Up | Generic. Every site says this. No perceived value. |
| Learn More | Vague destination. Does not promise anything specific. |
| Click Here | Meaningless. Does not describe value or action. |
| Get Started | Slightly better but still generic. Started with what? |
| Download | Better, but misses the chance to convey value. |

### Strong CTAs and Why They Work

| Strong CTA | Why It Works |
|------------|-------------|
| Start My Free Trial | First person ("My") creates ownership. "Free" reduces risk. |
| Get Your Personalized Plan | Specific deliverable. "Personalized" adds value. |
| See [Product] in Action | Promises a demo experience without commitment. |
| Create Your First [Thing] | "First" implies ease. Focuses on the user's outcome. |
| Try It Free -- No Account Needed | Stacks two objection removals: cost and friction. |
| Download the App | Clear action for mobile-specific pages. |
| Unlock Premium Features | "Unlock" implies the value is already there, waiting. |

### The CTA Formula

**[Action Verb] + [What They Get] + [Qualifier if needed]**

**Examples:**
- "Start My Free Trial" -- action (start) + value (free trial) + ownership (my)
- "Get the Complete Checklist" -- action (get) + specific deliverable (complete checklist)
- "See Pricing for My Team" -- action (see) + value (pricing) + context (my team)
- "Save My Spot" -- action (save) + ownership (my spot) + implied scarcity
- "Build My First Workout" -- action (build) + ownership (my) + specificity (first workout)

### CTA Best Practices

**Start with an action verb.** The first word should be a verb: Start, Get, Try, See, Build, Create, Download, Unlock, Join, Discover. This creates forward momentum.

**Be specific about the outcome.** "Get Started" could mean anything. "Get Your Sleep Report" tells the reader exactly what they are getting.

**Use first person strategically.** "Start My Free Trial" outperforms "Start Your Free Trial" in many tests because it creates a sense of ownership. However, test this -- it does not always win.

**Reduce risk near the CTA.** Place supporting text near the button: "No credit card required," "Cancel anytime," "Free for 14 days," "Takes 30 seconds." This is called a "CTA qualifier" and it addresses the last-second hesitation.

**Match the CTA to the commitment level.** Early on the page, use low-commitment CTAs ("See how it works"). At the bottom, after you have built the case, use higher-commitment CTAs ("Start my free trial").

### App-Specific CTA Guidance

**App store listing:** Your CTA is the "Get" or "Download" button, which you cannot customize. So your CTA work happens in the screenshots and preview text. End your description with a clear reason to download now.

**In-app upgrade prompts:** Keep them action-oriented and benefit-focused. "Unlock unlimited exports" is better than "Upgrade to Pro."

**Paywall screens:** The primary CTA should state the action and the value. "Start 7-Day Free Trial" works better than "Continue" or "Subscribe." Below the button, always clarify pricing and cancellation terms.

**Push notifications:** The CTA is the notification itself. Make it specific: "Your weekly report is ready -- see your progress" instead of "Check out the app."

## 6. Page Structure Framework

A well-structured page guides the reader through a persuasion sequence: attention, trust, understanding, desire, action. Each section has a job, and they work together like chapters in an argument.

### Above the Fold (First Screen)

The area visible without scrolling. You have roughly 3-5 seconds to communicate your value and earn the scroll.

**Headline:** Your single most important message. This is the core value proposition -- the one thing you want every visitor to walk away knowing. It should be specific, benefit-driven, and under 12 words.

**Subheadline:** Expands on the headline. If the headline is the hook, the subheadline is the explanation. It should add specificity, address a secondary concern, or provide a reason to believe. Keep it to 1-2 sentences.

**Before (weak above-the-fold):**
> Headline: "Welcome to TaskFlow"
> Subheadline: "The all-in-one productivity platform for modern teams"

**After (strong above-the-fold):**
> Headline: "Finish projects in half the time"
> Subheadline: "TaskFlow replaces your spreadsheets, status meetings, and Slack threads with one simple board. Used by 8,000 teams."

**Primary CTA:** Action-oriented button text. Communicate what they get, not what they do. "Start Free Trial" not "Sign Up." Position it prominently -- this is the most important button on the page.

**Supporting Visual:** Product screenshot, demo video, or hero image. The visual should reinforce the message, not compete with it. For apps, a clean device mockup showing the product in use is almost always better than an abstract illustration.

### Social Proof Section

Social proof answers the question "Should I trust this?" Position it early -- ideally right below the fold.

**Options (use 1-2 per placement):**
- **Customer logos:** Recognizable logos carry more weight than many logos. Five known brands beat twenty unknown ones.
- **Key metric:** "10,000+ teams," "4.8 stars from 23,000 reviews," "$2M saved for customers." Pick your most impressive, verifiable number.
- **Short testimonial:** A single powerful quote with attribution. "TaskFlow cut our meeting time in half." -- Sarah Kim, Head of Product at Lattice.
- **Star rating with review count:** "Rated 4.8 on the App Store (14,000+ ratings)." The combination of high rating and high volume is persuasive.

**Common mistake:** Burying social proof at the bottom of the page. By the time readers get there, many have already decided. Move your best proof near the top.

### Problem/Pain Section

This section proves you understand the reader's world. When done well, the reader thinks "This is exactly my problem." That recognition creates trust and makes them receptive to your solution.

**Structure:**
1. Open with recognition: "You know the feeling..." or "If you are like most [role]..."
2. Describe specific frustrations -- use vivid, concrete details, not abstractions
3. Hint at the cost of not solving: wasted time, missed opportunities, stress

**Before (generic problem section):**
> Managing tasks is hard. You have too many things to do and not enough time to do them.

**After (specific, resonant problem section):**
> It is Monday morning. You open your laptop to 47 unread messages, a project tracker that is three weeks out of date, and a status meeting in 20 minutes that you have not prepared for. You spend the next hour catching up instead of moving forward. By lunch, nothing important has actually gotten done.

**Why the "after" works:** It describes a specific, recognizable scenario with sensory details (Monday morning, laptop, 47 messages). The reader sees themselves in it.

### Solution/Benefits Section

Bridge from the problem to your solution. This is where you show how life changes with your product.

**Focus on 3-5 key benefits, not 10.** More is not better. Three compelling benefits with evidence beat ten generic claims.

**Format options:**
- **Benefit blocks:** Icon + headline + one-sentence explanation. Clean, scannable, effective.
- **Before/after comparison:** "Before [Product]" vs "After [Product]" with specific contrasts.
- **Feature-Benefit-Proof structure:** Name the feature, explain the benefit, prove it with a data point or quote.

**Example (Feature-Benefit-Proof):**

> **Smart Daily Planner**
> Automatically prioritizes your tasks based on deadlines, energy levels, and dependencies. You always know what to work on next.
> "I stopped spending 20 minutes every morning deciding what to do first." -- Mark T., Product Designer

### How It Works Section

This section reduces perceived complexity. Even if your product is sophisticated, present it as a simple process.

**Rules:**
- Use 3-4 steps maximum (odd numbers feel less complete, so 3 is ideal)
- Each step: simple action + outcome
- Include time estimates when possible (they set expectations and reduce anxiety)
- End with the result, not a step

**Example:**
1. "Download the app (free, 30 seconds)"
2. "Connect your calendar and task list"
3. "Get a smart daily plan every morning"

**Common mistake:** Making "How It Works" too technical. "Step 1: Configure your API integration" is a developer's how-it-works, not a customer's. Show the experience, not the implementation.

### Objection Handling Section

Every reader has reasons not to act. Your job is to identify and address those reasons before they become deal-breakers.

**Common objections and how to address them:**

| Objection | How to Address |
|-----------|---------------|
| "Is this right for my situation?" | Use cases, persona sections, "Built for [audience]" |
| "What if it does not work?" | Guarantee, free trial, testimonials with results |
| "Is it hard to set up?" | "How it works" section, time estimates, "no technical skills needed" |
| "How is this different from X?" | Comparison table, "Unlike [competitor], we..." |
| "It is too expensive" | ROI framing, "less than your daily coffee," pricing context |
| "I do not have time to learn something new" | Onboarding speed, "up and running in 2 minutes" |

**Formats:**
- **FAQ section:** Best for multiple smaller objections
- **Comparison table:** Best for competitive differentiation
- **Guarantee section:** Best for risk-related objections
- **"Built for [audience]":** Best for relevance objections

### Final CTA Section

The bottom of the page is for readers who made it all the way through. They are interested but may need one last push.

**Structure:**
1. Recap the value proposition in one line (not the same as the headline -- reframe it)
2. Repeat the primary CTA
3. Add risk reversal: guarantee, free trial, no credit card required
4. Optional: genuine urgency if it exists (a real deadline, limited spots)

**Before:**
> Ready to get started? Sign up now.

**After:**
> Join 12,000 teams who finish projects in half the time. Start your free trial today -- no credit card, cancel anytime.

## 7. Landing Page Section Variety

A landing page is not a feature list. It is a narrative. Each section type serves a different persuasive function, and variety keeps the reader engaged.

### Section Types

**How It Works (Numbered Steps):** Reduces complexity. Shows the path from "I am interested" to "I am getting value." Use 3-4 steps. Always end with the outcome, not the process.

**Competitor Comparison:** Position against the status quo or named competitors. Use a comparison table or "Unlike X, we..." framing. Be factual and fair -- readers respect honesty and distrust cheap shots.

**Founder Story / Our Story:** Emotional connection. Tells the reader why this product exists, what the founder was frustrated by, and what they believe. Differentiates from faceless competitors. Works especially well for indie apps.

**Testimonials:** Customer quotes with names, roles, and specific results. Quote cards, video testimonials, or tweet embeds. The more specific the result, the more persuasive: "Saved 5 hours a week" beats "Great app!"

**Case Studies:** Deeper customer success stories. Problem, solution, results format with specific metrics. Even a two-sentence case study is more persuasive than a generic testimonial.

**Use Cases:** Show different ways the product is used. Helps visitors self-identify: "Oh, people like me use this for the same thing I need." Organize by role, industry, or job-to-be-done.

**Personas / "Built For":** Explicitly name your audience: "Perfect for freelancers," "Designed for small marketing teams," "Built for parents, not productivity nerds." This inclusion/exclusion framing helps the right people feel welcome.

**Stats and Social Proof:** Key metrics that build credibility. Best when specific and verifiable: "4.8 stars from 23,000 reviews" not "Highly rated."

**FAQ:** Addresses objections and common questions. Good for SEO. Keep answers concise and confident -- do not hedge.

**Integrations:** Logo grid of tools you connect with. Answers "Will this work with my existing tools?" without requiring explanation.

**Guarantee / Risk Reversal:** Money-back guarantee, free trial terms, "cancel anytime." Reduces perceived risk. Position near CTAs for maximum effect.

### Example: Strong vs Weak Page Structure

**Feature-heavy page (weak):**
1. Hero
2. Feature 1
3. Feature 2
4. Feature 3
5. Feature 4
6. CTA

**Varied, persuasive page (strong):**
1. Hero with clear value prop
2. Social proof bar (logos or key stat)
3. Problem/pain section (empathy)
4. How it works (3 steps)
5. Key benefits (2-3 with evidence)
6. Testimonial with specific result
7. Use cases or personas
8. Comparison to alternatives
9. Case study snippet
10. FAQ (objection handling)
11. Final CTA with guarantee

The strong structure works because each section does a different job: earn attention, build trust, create empathy, demonstrate simplicity, prove value, address doubts, and drive action.

## 8. App Store & In-App Copy

Mobile app copy has unique constraints and opportunities. Character limits are strict, reading context is distracted, and the competition for attention is fierce.

### App Store Title and Subtitle

**iOS App Store:**
- Title: 30 characters max
- Subtitle: 30 characters max

**Google Play:**
- App name: 30 characters max
- Short description: 80 characters max

**Title rules:**
- Lead with the app name if it is descriptive ("Sleep Cycle," "Calm," "Headspace")
- If the name is not descriptive, use the subtitle to clarify
- Include your most important keyword naturally
- Do not keyword-stuff: "Meditation Sleep Calm Relax" is rejected by reviewers and alienates users

**Subtitle/short description rules:**
- State the core benefit in plain language
- Use the formula: [What it does] + [for whom or how]
- Example: "Guided sleep & meditation" (Calm)
- Example: "Daily planner & to-do list" (Structured)
- Example: "Budget tracker & bill reminder" (Mint)

**Before (weak):**
> Title: "FocusMate"
> Subtitle: "Productivity App"

**After (strong):**
> Title: "FocusMate"
> Subtitle: "Plan your day in 2 minutes"

### App Store Description

You get 4,000 characters on both iOS and Google Play, but only the first 1-3 lines are visible before "Read More." Those first lines must do all the persuasive work.

**First three lines (above the fold):**
- Open with your strongest value proposition
- Include a key proof point if possible
- Do not waste space on "Welcome to [App Name]"

**Before (wasted opening):**
> Welcome to FocusMate! We are so excited to share our app with you. FocusMate is a beautiful, powerful productivity tool designed to help you be your best self.

**After (strong opening):**
> Finish your to-do list before lunch. FocusMate plans your day in 2 minutes based on your deadlines, energy, and calendar. Used by 12,000 people who finally stopped overcommitting.

**Description body structure:**
1. Value proposition (lines 1-3)
2. How it works (3-4 bullet points)
3. Key features with benefits (not just feature names)
4. Social proof (rating, reviews, press)
5. Call to action to download/try

**Feature listing -- before:**
> - Cloud sync
> - Dark mode
> - Widgets
> - Calendar integration
> - 50+ templates

**Feature listing -- after:**
> - Syncs across all your devices -- start on your phone, finish on your iPad
> - Dark mode for late-night planning
> - Home screen widgets so your plan is always visible
> - Pulls in your calendar so you never double-book
> - 50+ templates for work, school, fitness, and personal goals

### App Store Screenshots

Screenshots are technically visual, but the text overlays are pure copywriting. Each screenshot should communicate one benefit with a short headline.

**Rules:**
- Lead with the most compelling screen (usually the core value prop, not the logo)
- One idea per screenshot
- Headlines should be benefit-driven: "See where your money goes" not "Dashboard view"
- Keep text to 5-8 words per screenshot headline
- Use the sequence to tell a story: Problem, Solution, Feature 1, Feature 2, Social proof

### In-App Onboarding Copy

Onboarding copy has one job: get the user to the "aha moment" as fast as possible. Every word that does not serve that goal is friction.

**Rules:**
- Keep each screen to one idea
- Use action-oriented language: "Choose your goal" not "Goal selection screen"
- Show progress (step 1 of 3)
- Explain why you are asking for information: "We ask your wake-up time to schedule your tasks at the right hours"
- End onboarding with a quick win, not a feature tour

**Before (feature tour onboarding):**
> Screen 1: "Welcome to FocusMate!"
> Screen 2: "We have task management"
> Screen 3: "We have calendar sync"
> Screen 4: "We have widgets"
> Screen 5: "Let's get started!"

**After (value-focused onboarding):**
> Screen 1: "What is your biggest productivity challenge?" [Pick one]
> Screen 2: "When do you do your best work?" [Morning/Afternoon/Evening]
> Screen 3: "Your first personalized plan is ready."

### Push Notification Copy

Push notifications are the most intimate and most abused surface in mobile marketing. One bad notification loses you a user forever. One good one brings them back.

**Rules:**
- Be useful, not promotional
- Be specific: "Your weekly sleep score is ready: 82/100" not "Check out your stats!"
- Respect timing: do not send at 2am unless it is a sleep app
- Never use ALL CAPS or multiple exclamation points
- Give users a reason to open: what will they see or learn?

**Before:**
> Don't forget to check the app today! We have new features waiting for you!

**After:**
> Your weekly focus report is ready. You had 3 more deep work hours than last week.

## 9. Paywall & Subscription Copy

Paywall screens are the highest-stakes copy in your app. This single screen determines whether a free user becomes a paying customer. Every word must earn its place.

### Paywall Headline

The headline should reframe the upgrade as a natural next step, not a toll booth.

**Before (transactional):**
> Upgrade to Premium

**After (benefit-driven):**
> Unlock your full plan

**More examples:**
- "Get the complete experience"
- "Your personal sleep coach, unlocked"
- "Everything you need to stay on track"
- "Remove limits. Build freely."

**Avoid:**
- "Buy now" (aggressive)
- "Go Pro" (vague -- pro at what?)
- "Premium Plan" as a headline (this is a label, not a value proposition)

### Paywall Feature List

Most paywalls list features. The best paywalls list benefits. Keep it to 3-5 items -- more than that overwhelms and dilutes.

**Before (feature list):**
> - Unlimited projects
> - Cloud sync
> - Advanced analytics
> - Priority support
> - Custom themes

**After (benefit list):**
> - Unlimited projects -- never hit a wall on what you can track
> - Sync everywhere -- your plan follows you across all devices
> - Smart insights -- see your productivity patterns and improve them
> - Custom themes -- make it yours

**Format tip:** Use checkmarks or icons, not bullet points. Visual markers make the list easier to scan and feel more like a checklist of things the user is "getting."

### Pricing Presentation

How you present the price matters as much as the price itself.

**Anchoring:** Show the monthly price next to the annual price. "9.99/month or 59.99/year (save 50%)" makes the annual plan feel like a deal.

**Daily framing:** "Less than $0.17/day" makes a $4.99/month subscription feel trivial. Use this for subscriptions over $3/month.

**Comparison framing:** "Less than a coffee a week" or "Less than one Uber ride a month." These work because they anchor against familiar, recurring expenses the reader does not think twice about.

**Trial framing:** "Try free for 7 days, then $4.99/month" is clear and honest. Always clarify what happens after the trial. Users who feel tricked cancel and leave one-star reviews.

### Paywall CTA

**Best practices:**
- State the action and the value: "Start Free Trial" not "Continue"
- If there is a trial, lead with it: "Try 7 Days Free"
- Below the button, clarify terms: "Cancel anytime. No charge until [date]."
- Include a "Not now" or "Maybe later" option -- forced paywalls without an escape create resentment

**Before:**
> [Subscribe]

**After:**
> [Start Your 7-Day Free Trial]
> No charge until April 1. Cancel anytime in Settings.

### Subscription Cancellation Prevention

When a user tries to cancel, your copy has one last chance. Do not beg. Do not guilt. Present value clearly and offer alternatives.

**Before:**
> Are you sure you want to cancel? You will lose access to all your data!

**After:**
> Before you go -- here is what you have accomplished with Premium this month:
> - 23 workouts completed
> - 4.2 hours of meditation
> - 12-day streak
>
> Want to pause instead? Keep your streak and data with a 1-month pause.

## 10. Copywriting Frameworks

Frameworks give your copy structure. They are not formulas to follow rigidly but mental models that ensure your argument is complete and logically ordered.

### PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

The most versatile framework. Works for landing pages, emails, ads, app store descriptions, and paywall copy.

**How it works:**

1. **Problem:** Identify the pain point your audience experiences. Be specific. Use their language.
2. **Agitate:** Amplify the consequences of the problem. What happens if they do not solve it? What does it cost them -- in time, money, stress, missed opportunities?
3. **Solution:** Present your product as the resolution. Connect it directly to the problem and agitation.

**Why it works:** PAS mirrors the way people make decisions. They notice a problem, feel the weight of it, and look for a way out. By the time you present your solution, the reader is primed to want it.

**Example -- Landing page:**

> **Problem:** You spend every Monday morning pulling numbers from four different dashboards into a spreadsheet.
>
> **Agitate:** That is 4 hours a week -- 200 hours a year -- on a report that is outdated by the time you send it. Meanwhile, your team is making decisions with last week's data.
>
> **Solution:** ReportFlow pulls data from every source, updates in real time, and sends a polished report to your team every Monday at 8am. You get your mornings back.

**Example -- App store description:**

> Tired of budgeting apps that make you feel worse about your money? (Problem) You download them full of hope, enter your expenses for a week, then quit because the red numbers are stressful. (Agitate) Calm Budget takes a different approach. It focuses on what you can spend, not what you have spent. Small wins every day instead of guilt. (Solution)

**Example -- Paywall:**

> Your free plan limits you to 3 projects. (Problem) That means choosing between tracking your work tasks, your side project, or your personal goals -- instead of managing all three. (Agitate) With Premium, there are no limits. Track everything in one place. (Solution)

### AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

Best for structured landing pages and long-form sales pages.

**How it works:**

1. **Attention:** Open with a bold claim, surprising statistic, or provocative question that makes the reader stop.
2. **Interest:** Build on the attention-grabber with details, context, and relevance. Show the reader this applies to them.
3. **Desire:** Shift from rational interest to emotional desire. Paint the picture of life with your product. Use benefits, testimonials, and social proof.
4. **Action:** Direct the reader to take a specific step. Remove friction and risk.

**Example -- Landing page:**

> **Attention:** 73% of to-do lists are never completed.
>
> **Interest:** The problem is not willpower. It is that most task apps let you add 50 items with no way to know what actually matters today. You end each day feeling behind, even when you worked all day.
>
> **Desire:** FocusMate plans your day based on your deadlines, energy levels, and calendar. Users report finishing 40% more tasks and feeling less stressed. "I finally feel like I am in control of my day." -- Sarah K.
>
> **Action:** Try FocusMate free for 14 days. No credit card required.

### StoryBrand

Best for brand positioning and homepage/about page copy. Based on Donald Miller's framework.

**How it works:**

1. **Hero (the customer)** has a problem
2. They meet a **Guide (you)** who understands
3. The Guide gives them a **Plan**
4. And calls them to **Action**
5. That results in **Success** and helps them avoid **Failure**

**Why it works:** It positions the customer as the hero and your product as the guide. This is psychologically powerful because people do not want to be sold to -- they want to be helped.

**Example -- Homepage:**

> **Hero:** You are a freelance designer juggling 6 clients, 14 projects, and zero time to chase invoices.
>
> **Guide:** We built InvoiceFlow because we were freelancers too. We know what it is like to finish great work and then spend hours on paperwork.
>
> **Plan:** Connect your project tool. We generate invoices automatically. Get paid on time.
>
> **Action:** Start your free trial.
>
> **Success:** Spend your time designing, not invoicing. Our users get paid an average of 11 days faster.
>
> **Failure avoided:** No more forgotten invoices. No more awkward "just following up" emails.

### When to Use Which Framework

| Situation | Best Framework |
|-----------|---------------|
| Short-form copy (ads, push, app store) | PAS |
| Landing page with single offer | AIDA |
| Homepage or brand page | StoryBrand |
| Paywall or upgrade prompt | PAS |
| Email sequence | AIDA |
| Feature page | PAS or AIDA |

## 11. Voice and Tone

Voice is your brand's personality -- it stays consistent. Tone is how that personality adjusts to context -- it shifts based on the situation.

### Establishing Voice

Before writing, define where your brand sits on these spectrums:

**Formality:**
- Casual/conversational ("Hey, let us fix your mornings")
- Professional but friendly ("A better way to plan your day")
- Formal/enterprise ("Comprehensive project management for distributed teams")

**Personality:**
- Playful or serious?
- Bold or understated?
- Technical or accessible?
- Warm or authoritative?

### Adjusting Tone by Context

The same brand voice should feel different in different contexts:

| Surface | Tone Adjustment |
|---------|----------------|
| Landing page headline | Bolder, more assertive |
| App store description | Clear, benefit-focused, slightly more formal |
| In-app onboarding | Warm, encouraging, helpful |
| Push notification | Casual, brief, useful |
| Paywall | Confident, value-focused, not pushy |
| Error message | Empathetic, constructive, clear |
| Cancellation flow | Respectful, non-manipulative |

### Consistency Rules

- Pick a person (first person "we," second person "you," third person "it") and stick with it within a page
- Maintain the same level of formality throughout a single surface
- Headlines can be bolder than body copy, but they should not feel like they come from a different brand
- If you use humor, use it consistently -- one joke on an otherwise serious page feels jarring

### Common Voice Mistakes in App Marketing

- **Startup voice that tries too hard:** "We are absolutely obsessed with productivity. Like, seriously obsessed." Just state what you do.
- **Enterprise voice on a consumer app:** "Our comprehensive solution facilitates optimal workflow management." You are a to-do app. Talk like a person.
- **Inconsistent voice:** Landing page says "Hey, let us help you rock your mornings!" but the app itself says "Please configure your notification preferences." Pick a lane.
- **Copying a successful brand's voice without their context:** Slack's casual voice works because they had years of product-market fit before they leaned into it. You may need to earn trust with clarity before you can be cute.

## 12. Best Practices Checklist

Use this as a final review before publishing any marketing copy. Each item addresses a specific failure mode.

### Value and Positioning
- [ ] Leads with value to the customer, not features of the product
- [ ] Core value proposition is clear within the first 5 seconds of reading
- [ ] Differentiator from alternatives is stated or implied
- [ ] Target audience can identify themselves in the copy

### Persuasion and Structure
- [ ] Addresses at least 2-3 target audience pain points
- [ ] Uses both emotional and logical appeals
- [ ] Builds a logical flow: attention, trust, understanding, desire, action
- [ ] Each section advances one idea (not overloaded)
- [ ] Social proof is present and specific (not generic)
- [ ] Objections are handled proactively

### Calls to Action
- [ ] Primary CTA is clear, specific, and benefit-oriented
- [ ] CTA appears multiple times on longer pages
- [ ] CTA button text starts with an action verb
- [ ] Risk-reduction language appears near CTAs (free trial, guarantee, no credit card)

### Writing Quality
- [ ] Active voice throughout (passive used only when intentional)
- [ ] Present tense (not future -- "Get results" not "You will get results")
- [ ] Specific numbers and data replace vague claims
- [ ] Customer language used (not internal/company jargon)
- [ ] "You/your" outnumbers "we/our"
- [ ] No hedging words (almost, very, really, basically)
- [ ] No banned buzzwords without substance (innovative, seamless, robust, cutting-edge)
- [ ] No exclamation points

### Readability
- [ ] Scannable -- uses headers, bullets, short paragraphs
- [ ] No sentence exceeds 25 words
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 4 lines
- [ ] Technical terms are explained or avoided
- [ ] Reads naturally when spoken aloud

### Integrity
- [ ] No fabricated statistics or claims
- [ ] No fake urgency or manufactured scarcity
- [ ] Testimonials are real and attributed
- [ ] Screenshots show actual product state
- [ ] Pricing and trial terms are transparent
