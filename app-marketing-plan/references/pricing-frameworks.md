## 1. Core Principle

**Charge something. Always.** There is a massive difference between free and $1. Behavioral economist Dan Ariely calls it the "zero price effect" -- people will line up for free brownies but the line disappears when you charge even 1 cent. If you don't charge, you can't learn what customers actually value.

## 2. Pricing Models

### Cost-Based Pricing
- Calculate costs (hosting, time, materials, payment processing)
- Add a margin (20-50% typical)
- Example: Retail stores buy wholesale and double the price (50% margin)
- Best for: physical products, services with clear costs

### Value-Based Pricing
- Price based on value to the customer, not your costs
- A feature might cost nothing extra to deliver but be worth a lot
- Example: Netflix's multi-screen feature costs nothing but they charge a premium
- Best for: software, digital products, services with high perceived value

## 3. Pricing Principles

1. **Start low, raise over time.** Prices generally go up as products improve. That's expected and healthy.
2. **Pricing is not permanent.** It's another thing to iterate on. Start the discovery process, don't aim for perfection.
3. **Tiered pricing is the goal.** Like plane tickets -- economy, business, first class. Same destination, different experience.
4. **The zero price effect.** Never give your product away for free as your default.
5. **Free trials are table stakes.** Customers open six tabs and compare immediately. Offer trials, but with a clear path to paid.
6. **Don't confuse marketing with giving away your product.** Ad-driven models make it hard to start charging later.

## 4. Initial Price Setting

Ask these questions:
1. What are your variable costs per unit/customer?
2. What are competing/alternative solutions charging?
3. What would make this a "no-brainer" purchase for your ideal customer?
4. What price lets you be profitable from customer #1?

## 5. Financial Independence Math

Calculate:
- How much do you need per month to sustain yourself?
- At your price point, how many customers is that?
- At one new customer per business day (260/year), when do you hit that number?

**Worked example:**
$10/month product, need $2,000/month = 200 customers = less than 1 year at 1 customer/day

## 6. Tiered Pricing Progression

### When to Introduce Tiers
- After you understand your customer segments (typically after 50-100 customers)
- When you notice distinct user groups with different willingness to pay
- When you have features worth differentiating on

### Tier Structure
- **Free/Basic**: Core value, enough to reach aha moment. Used as acquisition funnel.
- **Pro/Standard**: Full features for serious users. This is your bread and butter.
- **Premium/Enterprise**: Advanced features, priority support, team features. Higher margin.

### Feature Allocation
- Free tier: Enough to experience value, limited by usage (projects, storage, exports)
- Paid tier: Remove limits, add productivity features
- Premium: Add collaboration, analytics, integrations, priority support

## 7. Concentric Circles Sales Strategy

Sell outward from people who care most to people who care least:

### Circle 1: Friends and Family
- Start here. Yes, it's uncomfortable. Do it anyway.
- Pitch them on being first customers, not investors
- Ask for honest feedback, not social media posts

### Circle 2: Your Community
- Subject matter experts who understand the problem
- Make a list of everyone who has shared anything about a similar business
- Contact them all personally -- walk them through your product
- Ask for candid feedback

### Circle 3: Strangers (Cold Outreach)
- Cold emails, calls, messages -- this works
- Personalize each message. Don't copy/paste.
- Use each rejection as a learning opportunity

**Example cold email:**
> "Hi [Name], I saw you're [doing X the hard way]. I built [product] which basically automates all of this. I'd love to show it to you, or you can check it out yourself: [link]. Also happy to just share any learnings we see from [community]. Let me know!"

## 8. When to Revisit Pricing

Signals it's time to adjust:
- You haven't raised prices in 6+ months and product has improved significantly
- Conversion rate is unusually high (price may be too low)
- Customers never mention price as an objection
- You're adding significant new features
- Competitor pricing has shifted
- You're attracting wrong customer segment (too cheap attracts low-intent users)
